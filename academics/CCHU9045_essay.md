Perception is an important part of personality. The way people experience the world affects what they notice, what they avoid, and how they respond to stress. For neurodivergent people, differences in attention, sensory processing, and social understanding can shape daily habits and long-term personality development (Wrzus & Roberts, 2017).

When people talk about perception, they often think only about the five senses. In fact, perception also includes how we experience time, how we deal with background noise, how we read social cues, and how we understand our own physical feelings. These experiences do not determine personality by themselves, but they can influence how a person adapts to the world. Over time, repeated experiences of comfort, stress, misunderstanding, or acceptance can shape values, coping styles, and behavior (Wrzus & Roberts, 2017).

In this essay, I use three personal examples: myself and two close friends. These examples cannot represent every neurodivergent person. However, they show how different ways of perceiving the world can influence personality in different ways.

### The Flood of Information: My ADHD Perspective

I experience the world through ADHD(Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder). For me, one major issue is difficulty filtering sensory input. In busy places, I often notice too many sounds and small changes at once. Another issue is my weak sense of time. I can lose track of hours very easily, especially when I am focused. This personal experience is consistent with research showing atypical time perception and sensory gating in many adults with ADHD (Mette, 2023; Micoulaud-Franchi et al., 2015).

Physical restlessness, such as incessantly shaking legs, twirling a pen, biting fingernails, are common. This behavior serves as a way to wake up the brain, which is under-stimulated. Due to low dopamine levels, the brain exists in a state of sluggishness or boredom, so my body creates physical movement to self-stimulate and barely maintain attention on dull tasks on lectures.

At the same time, ADHD also makes me seek stimulation. Complex technical work, such as learning psychiatry or doing hand crafting, can hold my attention for a very long time, like for 4-8 hours researching just for interests, but not continuously for days. While other people may find these tasks tiring, I often become deeply focused when the problem is difficult and interesting for me enough. In those moments, I may forget the time completely and keep working late without noticing.

This pattern has shaped my personality. Because ordinary routines can feel difficult, I have often felt frustrated with myself. However, dealing with this constant mental pressure has also made me flexible and quick to respond in stressful situations. I have become more spontaneous and more willing to solve problems in unusual ways.

### The Architecture of Order: An ASD Perspective

Laview is a 17-year-old autistic transgender woman who was born in Japan and now lives in Shenzhen. She experiences the world with strong sensory sensitivity. Noise, touch, and visual clutter can become overwhelming very quickly. Research on autism has consistently reported atypical sensory modulation and sensory sensitivity in many autistic people (Ben-Sasson et al., 2009). At the same time, she also has to deal with social pressure as both an autistic person and a transgender woman. Because of this, everyday life can be exhausting for her.

She often has to hide some of her autistic traits in order to fit into spaces built around neurotypical expectations. She also faces situations where other people question or misunderstand her gender identity. These pressures do not define her whole life, but they do create stress, burnout, and social withdrawal. Over time, this has made her more careful and more protective of her energy.

For Laview, gender dysphoria is connected not only to identity but also to sensory discomfort. She describes facial stubble as physically unbearable, not just emotionally upsetting. This shows how sensory sensitivity can intensify an already painful experience. Research has also reported elevated co-occurrence between autism and gender dysphoria or gender incongruence, although this does not mean one directly causes the other (Kallitsounaki & Williams, 2023). She also describes herself as asexual. It is better to treat this as her personal experience rather than as a universal feature of autistic people. In relationships, she values emotional closeness, but physical intimacy can feel stressful and overwhelming.

These experiences have shaped her personality in clear ways. She is very direct, honest, and resistant to social hypocrisy. Because predictability helps her manage sensory overload, she prefers routine, reliability, and clear logic. This preference for sameness and predictability is also discussed in autism research (Gotham et al., 2013). She also prefers an androgynous style that feels comfortable and true to herself instead of trying to follow traditional expectations of femininity.

### The Triple Masking: An AuDHD (Autism and ADHD) Perspective

Chinami is a 22-year-old transgender woman with both autism and ADHD, studying at the City University of Hong Kong. Her experience is shaped by contradiction. One part of her wants novelty, stimulation, and change. Another part wants routine, control, and predictability. These two tendencies can clash with each other in daily life.

When a person has both Autism and ADHD, their brain is like having both the gas and brake pedals pressed at the same time. On one hand, the autistic part strives to create perfect plans and adhere to strict routines. on the other, the ADHD side—struggling with low dopamine levels—is unable to execute these tasks or feels overwhelmed by such rigidity. For example, the autistic side is constantly decoding complex social rules, treating everything like a logic puzzle. Meanwhile, the ADHD side operates with poor impulse control—it knows the answer but cannot hit the pause button. The resulting state is someone who is overthinking every subtle clue (by ASD) only to respond with a thought that feels too immediate or disruptive (by ADHD).

To manage the cognitive load, she often has to simultaneously perform multiple functions during conversations across three domains, like suppressing her autistic traits, managing ADHD impulsivity, and navigating her transgender identity. She may try not to interrupt, pay attention to eye contact, interpret hidden meanings, and adjust her voice or posture. She often needs to engage in triple masking when communicating with others, concealing her autism, ADHD, and transgender background from a general audience. It is tough, but high intelligence help her to manage these masking. She is lucky, but not for every neurodivergent people can have and do this.

Research suggests that camouflaging is associated with poorer mental health outcomes in autistic people, and autistic burnout is often described as the result of long periods of strain and self-monitoring (Khudiakova et al., 2024; Mantzalas et al., 2022). At the same time, this effort has also made her highly observant and emotionally aware. She can read social situations quickly and adjust to them when necessary.

As a result, her personality has become both adaptive and cautious. She is empathetic and sensitive to other people’s feelings, but she also protects herself because she is used to being watched and judged. Her personality shows how constant adjustment can produce both social skill and emotional exhaustion.

### The Social Mirror and Conclusion

These examples show that personality does not develop in isolation. Social response matters. When people are repeatedly misunderstood, doubted, or judged, they may become guarded, anxious, or withdrawn. When they are respected and supported, they may become more confident and open. In this way, society helps shape personality by shaping which traits feel safe to express (Wrzus & Roberts, 2017).

It is also important not to make the opposite mistake and treat neurotypical people as all the same. Neurotypical people do not all have one personality, and neurodivergent people do not all share one life path. Still, people whose behavior fits social expectations often experience less friction in daily life. People whose perception or behavior is seen as unusual may face more pressure to explain themselves.

In some cases, this pressure appears in medical or institutional settings. Studies show that autistic adults can face communication, sensory, and system-level barriers in healthcare, while many transgender adults also face structural barriers when seeking gender-related care (Arnold et al., 2024; White Hughto et al., 2017). When this happens, it can damage trust and self-esteem. It can also make people feel that they must constantly prove their identity and their struggles to others.

In conclusion, personality is shaped by biology, life experience, and the social environment together. For neurodivergent people, perceptual experience can play a major role because it changes how everyday life feels from the inside. The three examples in this essay show that different perceptual styles can lead to different coping methods, values, and social habits. Understanding this does not mean reducing people to diagnoses. It means taking lived experience seriously and recognizing that different ways of perceiving the world can produce equally valid ways of being a person.
