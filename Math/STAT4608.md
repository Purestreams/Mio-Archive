## Chapter 1 & 2: Fundamentals of Risk and Returns


### Risk Measures
Standard deviation ($\sigma$) or variance is a primary measure of randomness or volatility. The Minkowski inequality shows that the volatility of a portfolio is smaller than the sum of individual asset volatilities, illustrating diversification.

### Basel Accords
Regulators (BCBS) use the Basel Accords to control risk-taking by enforcing capital adequacy requirements. Total risk charge combines Credit Risk Charge (CRC) and Market Risk Charge (MRC).

### Returns
- **Simple Return:** $R_t = \frac{V_t - V_{t-1}}{V_{t-1}}$



- **Log (Continuous) Return:** $r_t = \ln(\frac{V_t}{V_{t-1}}) = \ln(1 + R_t)$. Log returns are preferred in econometrics due to their additive properties over time.

### Distribution Shape
- **Skewness:** Measures asymmetry. Financial returns are often negatively skewed.
- **Kurtosis:** Measures tail thickness. Excess kurtosis is $\kappa - 3$. Positive excess kurtosis indicates fatter tails than a normal distribution.

## Chapter 3: Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Expected Shortfall (ES)

Value-at-Risk summarizes the worst loss over a target horizon at a given confidence level $C$ (where significance level $\alpha = 1 - C$).

### Two Types of VaR
- **Absolute VaR:** Measures loss relative to the initial value: $VaR(0) = V_0 - V^* = -V_0 R^*$.
- **Relative VaR:** Measures loss relative to the expected mean value: $VaR(\mu) = \mathbb{E}(V) - V^* = V_0(\mu - R^*)$.





### Normal Model for VaR
Assuming returns $R \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$:
- $VaR(\mu) = V_0 Z_\alpha \sigma$
- $VaR(0) = VaR(\mu) - V_0 \mu$




### Expected Shortfall (ES)
The conditional expected loss given that the loss exceeds VaR.
- Under the normal model: $ES = \frac{V_0 \sigma \phi(-Z_\alpha)}{\alpha} - V_0 \mu$ (where $\phi$ is the standard normal pdf).




### Stochastic Process (SP) Model
Models asset price paths in continuous time.
- Accounting for continuous dividend yield $\delta$: $S_T = S_0 \exp\{(\mu - \delta - \frac{\sigma^2}{2})T + \sigma\sqrt{T}Z\}$.




### Time Scaling
For i.i.d. returns over $\Delta t$, variance scales linearly ($\sigma^2 \Delta t$), so volatility scales by $\sqrt{\Delta t}$. Thus, $VaR(\mu, \Delta t) = VaR(\mu) \sqrt{\Delta t}$.

## Chapter 4: Backtesting VaR Models

Backtesting validates a model by verifying if actual losses align with projected losses.

### Z-Test
A simple frequency test. If $N$ is the number of VaR exceptions over $T$ days, $Z = \frac{N - pT}{\sqrt{p(1-p)T}}$ (where $p = 1 - C$).

### Likelihood Ratio (LR) Tests
- **Unconditional Coverage ($LR_{uc}$):** Tests if the overall frequency of exceptions matches $p$. $LR_{uc} = -2 \ln \left[ \frac{(1-p)^{T-N} p^N}{(1-\hat{p})^{T-N} \hat{p}^N} \right] \sim \chi^2(1)$.


- **Independence Test ($LR_{ind}$):** Tests if exceptions cluster sequentially (e.g., exceptions following previous exceptions).
- **Conditional Coverage ($LR_{cc}$):** $LR_{cc} = LR_{uc} + LR_{ind} \sim \chi^2(2)$.




### Normality Testing
Since financial data is rarely perfectly normal, tests like Jarque-Bera check for deviations using sample skewness ($\hat{\eta}$) and kurtosis ($\hat{\kappa}$).

$$JB = T \left( \frac{\hat{\eta}^2}{6} + \frac{(\hat{\kappa}-3)^2}{24} \right) \sim \chi^2(2)$$

## Chapter 5: Portfolio VaR and Risk Decomposition

Portfolio VaR takes into account the diversification benefits of non-perfectly correlated assets.

### Portfolio Variance
For a portfolio vector of weights $w$ and covariance matrix $\Sigma$: $\sigma_p^2 = w^{\prime}\Sigma w$. For dollar amounts $x = w V_0$, the dollar variance is $\sigma_v^2 = x^{\prime}\Sigma x$.

### Diversified vs. Undiversified VaR
- **Diversified:** $VaR_p = Z_\alpha \sqrt{x^{\prime}\Sigma x}$
- **Undiversified:** The simple sum of individual VaRs: $\sum Z_\alpha V_0 \sigma_i |w_i|$. Due to the Minkowski inequality, Diversified VaR $\le$ Undiversified VaR.




### Risk Decomposition Tools
- **Beta ($\beta_i$):** Measures systematic risk contribution: $\beta_i = \frac{Cov(R_i, R_p)}{\sigma_p^2}$.
- **Marginal VaR ($\Delta VaR_i$):** The change in portfolio VaR per percentage change in weight: $\Delta VaR_i = \frac{VaR_p}{V_0} \beta_i$.
- **Incremental VaR (IVaR):** The change in VaR for adding a new position $a$. Approximation: $IVaR \approx (\Delta VaR)^{\prime}a$.
- **Component VaR ($CVaR_i$):** How much risk asset $i$ contributes to the total VaR. $CVaR_i = VaR_p \beta_i w_i$. The sum of all Component VaRs exactly equals the Portfolio VaR.

## Sample Question: Portfolio VaR and Component VaR

**Question:** You manage a $10 million portfolio invested in two assets: $6 million in Asset X and $4 million in Asset Y. The daily volatility is 1.5% for Asset X and 2.0% for Asset Y. The correlation between their returns is 0.4. Assume a normal distribution and a mean return of 0. Calculate the following at a 99% confidence level ($Z = 2.326$):

1. The Undiversified Portfolio VaR.
2. The Diversified Portfolio VaR.
3. The Component VaR of Asset X.

**Solution:**
**1. Undiversified VaR**
Calculate individual VaRs:

* $VaR_X = 2.326 \times 0.015 \times \$6,000,000 = \$209,340$
* $VaR_Y = 2.326 \times 0.020 \times \$4,000,000 = \$186,080$
* Undiversified VaR $= \$209,340 + \$186,080 =$ **$395,420**

**2. Diversified Portfolio VaR**
First, calculate the portfolio variance in dollar terms ($\sigma_v^2$):


$$\sigma_v^2 = x_X^2 \sigma_X^2 + x_Y^2 \sigma_Y^2 + 2 x_X x_Y \rho \sigma_X \sigma_Y$$

$$\sigma_v^2 = (6M)^2(0.015)^2 + (4M)^2(0.020)^2 + 2(6M)(4M)(0.4)(0.015)(0.020)$$

$$\sigma_v^2 = 8,100,000 + 6,400,000 + 5,760,000 = 20,260,000$$


Next, find portfolio dollar volatility ($\sigma_v$):


$$\sigma_v = \sqrt{20,260,000} \approx \$4,501.11$$


Diversified VaR $= 2.326 \times 4,501.11 \approx$ **$10,469.58** *(Wait, variance calculation error check: $(6,000,000 \times 0.015)^2 = 90,000^2 = 8,100,000,000$. Let's use millions as units to simplify)*:


$$\sigma_v^2 = 6^2(0.015)^2 + 4^2(0.020)^2 + 2(6)(4)(0.4)(0.015)(0.020)$$

$$\sigma_v^2 = 0.0081 + 0.0064 + 0.00576 = 0.02026$$

$$\sigma_v = \sqrt{0.02026} \approx 0.142337 \text{ million} = \$142,337.63$$


Diversified VaR $= 2.326 \times \$142,337.63 =$ **$331,077.32**

**3. Component VaR of Asset X**
We need the covariance of X with the portfolio: $Cov(R_X, R_p)$.
Weights: $w_X = 0.6$, $w_Y = 0.4$.


$$Cov(R_X, R_p) = w_X \sigma_X^2 + w_Y \sigma_{XY}$$

$$Cov(R_X, R_p) = 0.6(0.015)^2 + 0.4(0.4 \times 0.015 \times 0.020) = 0.000135 + 0.000048 = 0.000183$$


Portfolio variance ($\sigma_p^2$): $\frac{0.02026}{10^2} = 0.0002026$
Calculate Beta ($\beta_X$):


$$\beta_X = \frac{0.000183}{0.0002026} \approx 0.90326$$


Calculate Component VaR:


$$CVaR_X = VaR_p \times \beta_X \times w_X = \$331,077.32 \times 0.90326 \times 0.6 \approx \mathbf{\$179,428.87}$$
