## Chapter 1 & 2: Fundamentals of Risk and Returns


### Risk Measures
Standard deviation ($\sigma$) or variance is a primary measure of randomness or volatility. The Minkowski inequality shows that the volatility of a portfolio is smaller than the sum of individual asset volatilities, illustrating diversification.

### Basel Accords
Regulators (BCBS) use the Basel Accords to control risk-taking by enforcing capital adequacy requirements. Total risk charge combines Credit Risk Charge (CRC) and Market Risk Charge (MRC).

### Returns
- **Simple Return:** $R_t = \frac{V_t - V_{t-1}}{V_{t-1}}$



- **Log (Continuous) Return:** $r_t = \ln(\frac{V_t}{V_{t-1}}) = \ln(1 + R_t)$. Log returns are preferred in econometrics due to their additive properties over time.

### Distribution Shape
- **Skewness:** Measures asymmetry. Financial returns are often negatively skewed.
- **Kurtosis:** Measures tail thickness. Excess kurtosis is $\kappa - 3$. Positive excess kurtosis indicates fatter tails than a normal distribution.

## Chapter 3: Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Expected Shortfall (ES)

Value-at-Risk summarizes the worst loss over a target horizon at a given confidence level $C$ (where significance level $\alpha = 1 - C$).

### Two Types of VaR
- **Absolute VaR:** Measures loss relative to the initial value: $VaR(0) = V_0 - V^* = -V_0 R^*$.
- **Relative VaR:** Measures loss relative to the expected mean value: $VaR(\mu) = \mathbb{E}(V) - V^* = V_0(\mu - R^*)$.





### Normal Model for VaR
Assuming returns $R \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$:
- $VaR(\mu) = V_0 Z_\alpha \sigma$
- $VaR(0) = VaR(\mu) - V_0 \mu$




### Expected Shortfall (ES)
The conditional expected loss given that the loss exceeds VaR.
- Under the normal model: $ES = \frac{V_0 \sigma \phi(-Z_\alpha)}{\alpha} - V_0 \mu$ (where $\phi$ is the standard normal pdf).




### Stochastic Process (SP) Model
Models asset price paths in continuous time.
- Accounting for continuous dividend yield $\delta$: $S_T = S_0 \exp\{(\mu - \delta - \frac{\sigma^2}{2})T + \sigma\sqrt{T}Z\}$.




### Time Scaling
For i.i.d. returns over $\Delta t$, variance scales linearly ($\sigma^2 \Delta t$), so volatility scales by $\sqrt{\Delta t}$. Thus, $VaR(\mu, \Delta t) = VaR(\mu) \sqrt{\Delta t}$.

## Chapter 4: Backtesting VaR Models

Backtesting validates a model by verifying if actual losses align with projected losses.

### Z-Test
A simple frequency test. If $N$ is the number of VaR exceptions over $T$ days, $Z = \frac{N - pT}{\sqrt{p(1-p)T}}$ (where $p = 1 - C$).

### Likelihood Ratio (LR) Tests
- **Unconditional Coverage ($LR_{uc}$):** Tests if the overall frequency of exceptions matches $p$. $LR_{uc} = -2 \ln \left[ \frac{(1-p)^{T-N} p^N}{(1-\hat{p})^{T-N} \hat{p}^N} \right] \sim \chi^2(1)$.


- **Independence Test ($LR_{ind}$):** Tests if exceptions cluster sequentially (e.g., exceptions following previous exceptions).
- **Conditional Coverage ($LR_{cc}$):** $LR_{cc} = LR_{uc} + LR_{ind} \sim \chi^2(2)$.




### Normality Testing
Since financial data is rarely perfectly normal, tests like Jarque-Bera check for deviations using sample skewness ($\hat{\eta}$) and kurtosis ($\hat{\kappa}$).

$$JB = T \left( \frac{\hat{\eta}^2}{6} + \frac{(\hat{\kappa}-3)^2}{24} \right) \sim \chi^2(2)$$

## Chapter 5: Portfolio VaR and Risk Decomposition

Portfolio VaR takes into account the diversification benefits of non-perfectly correlated assets.

### Portfolio Variance
For a portfolio vector of weights $w$ and covariance matrix $\Sigma$: $\sigma_p^2 = w^{\prime}\Sigma w$. For dollar amounts $x = w V_0$, the dollar variance is $\sigma_v^2 = x^{\prime}\Sigma x$.

### Diversified vs. Undiversified VaR
- **Diversified:** $VaR_p = Z_\alpha \sqrt{x^{\prime}\Sigma x}$
- **Undiversified:** The simple sum of individual VaRs: $\sum Z_\alpha V_0 \sigma_i |w_i|$. Due to the Minkowski inequality, Diversified VaR $\le$ Undiversified VaR.




### Risk Decomposition Tools
- **Beta ($\beta_i$):** Measures systematic risk contribution: $\beta_i = \frac{Cov(R_i, R_p)}{\sigma_p^2}$.
- **Marginal VaR ($\Delta VaR_i$):** The change in portfolio VaR per percentage change in weight: $\Delta VaR_i = \frac{VaR_p}{V_0} \beta_i$.
- **Incremental VaR (IVaR):** The change in VaR for adding a new position $a$. Approximation: $IVaR \approx (\Delta VaR)^{\prime}a$.
- **Component VaR ($CVaR_i$):** How much risk asset $i$ contributes to the total VaR. $CVaR_i = VaR_p \beta_i w_i$. The sum of all Component VaRs exactly equals the Portfolio VaR.

## Sample Question: Portfolio VaR and Component VaR

**Question:** You manage a $10 million portfolio invested in two assets: $6 million in Asset X and $4 million in Asset Y. The daily volatility is 1.5% for Asset X and 2.0% for Asset Y. The correlation between their returns is 0.4. Assume a normal distribution and a mean return of 0. Calculate the following at a 99% confidence level ($Z = 2.326$):

1. The Undiversified Portfolio VaR.
2. The Diversified Portfolio VaR.
3. The Component VaR of Asset X.

**Solution:**
**1. Undiversified VaR**
Calculate individual VaRs:

* $VaR_X = 2.326 \times 0.015 \times \$6,000,000 = \$209,340$
* $VaR_Y = 2.326 \times 0.020 \times \$4,000,000 = \$186,080$
* Undiversified VaR $= \$209,340 + \$186,080 =$ **$395,420**

**2. Diversified Portfolio VaR**
First, calculate the portfolio variance in dollar terms ($\sigma_v^2$):


$$\sigma_v^2 = x_X^2 \sigma_X^2 + x_Y^2 \sigma_Y^2 + 2 x_X x_Y \rho \sigma_X \sigma_Y$$

$$\sigma_v^2 = (6,000,000 \times 0.015)^2 + (4,000,000 \times 0.020)^2 + 2(6,000,000)(4,000,000)(0.4)(0.015)(0.020)$$

$$\sigma_v^2 = 8{,}100{,}000{,}000 + 6{,}400{,}000{,}000 + 5{,}760{,}000{,}000 = 20{,}260{,}000{,}000$$


Next, find portfolio dollar volatility ($\sigma_v$):


$$\sigma_v = \sqrt{20{,}260{,}000{,}000} \approx \$142{,}337.63$$


Diversified VaR $= 2.326 \times \$142{,}337.63 =$ **$331,077.32**

**3. Component VaR of Asset X**
We need the covariance of X with the portfolio: $Cov(R_X, R_p)$.
Weights: $w_X = 0.6$, $w_Y = 0.4$.


$$Cov(R_X, R_p) = w_X \sigma_X^2 + w_Y \sigma_{XY}$$

$$Cov(R_X, R_p) = 0.6(0.015)^2 + 0.4(0.4 \times 0.015 \times 0.020) = 0.000135 + 0.000048 = 0.000183$$


Portfolio variance in return terms:


$$\sigma_p^2 = 0.6^2(0.015)^2 + 0.4^2(0.020)^2 + 2(0.6)(0.4)(0.4)(0.015)(0.020) = 0.0002026$$


Calculate Beta ($\beta_X$):


$$\beta_X = \frac{0.000183}{0.0002026} \approx 0.90326$$


Calculate Component VaR:


$$CVaR_X = VaR_p \times \beta_X \times w_X = \$331,077.32 \times 0.90326 \times 0.6 \approx \mathbf{\$179,428.87}$$


Quick check: $CVaR_Y = \$331,077.32 - \$179,428.87 = \$151,648.45$, and the two component VaRs add up to the total portfolio VaR.

## Chapter 6: Simulation Approaches

Simulation methods are useful when analytical formulas are hard to apply, when returns are not well described by a normal model, or when we want to study hypothetical extreme scenarios.

### Why Simulation?
- It can handle complicated payoff functions, such as portfolios with options.
- It can model heavy-tailed or non-normal returns.
- It allows us to generate many possible future scenarios and then estimate VaR or ES from the simulated outcomes.

### Basic Monte Carlo Workflow
1. Specify a model for the risk factor or asset return.
2. Generate many random draws from that model.
3. Revalue the asset or portfolio under each draw.
4. Sort the simulated profits and losses.
5. Read off VaR from the required quantile and ES from the average of the tail losses.

### Simulating a Single Asset Return
If $X \sim t(\nu)$ with $\nu > 2$, then

$$Var(X) = \frac{\nu}{\nu - 2}$$


To create a return with target daily mean $\mu_d$ and daily volatility $\sigma_d$, use


$$R = \mu_d + \sigma_d \frac{X}{\sqrt{\nu/(\nu-2)}}$$


This guarantees that $E(R) = \mu_d$ and $Var(R) = \sigma_d^2$.

For the common case $X \sim t(4)$, we have $Var(X) = 2$, so


$$R = \mu_d + \sigma_d \frac{X}{\sqrt{2}}$$


### Simulating Correlated Assets
If $Z \sim N(0, I)$ and the covariance matrix is $\Sigma = LL^{\prime}$ from the Cholesky decomposition, then correlated normal returns can be simulated by


$$R = \mu + LZ$$


This is the standard way to preserve correlations in a multivariate Monte Carlo simulation.

### Quick Example: Simulating One Return
**Question:** Suppose $X \sim t(4)$, the daily mean is $\mu_d = 0.001$, and the daily volatility is $\sigma_d = 0.02$. If one simulated draw is $X = -1.5$, what is the simulated daily return?

**Solution:** Since $Var(X) = 2$, use


$$R = 0.001 + 0.02 \frac{X}{\sqrt{2}}$$


Substituting $X = -1.5$:


$$R = 0.001 + 0.02 \times \frac{-1.5}{\sqrt{2}} \approx -0.0202$$


So the simulated return is about **$-2.02\%$**.

### Quick Example: VaR from Simulated Losses
**Question:** A portfolio produces the following simulated one-day losses (in dollars):

$10{,}000$, $15{,}000$, $18{,}000$, $25{,}000$, $40{,}000$, $55{,}000$, $70{,}000$, $90{,}000$, $110{,}000$, $150{,}000$.

Using this toy sample, estimate the 90% VaR.

**Solution:** Order the losses from smallest to largest. With 10 scenarios, the 90th percentile is the 9th value.

So the estimated **90% VaR is $\$110{,}000$**.

The intuition is simple: only about 10% of the simulated scenarios are worse than this loss.

## Chapter 7: Models for Conditional VaR

Conditional VaR updates risk estimates using the most recent information. This is important because financial volatility is not constant over time and often clusters.

### Conditional Mean and Variance
Given past information $\mathcal{F}_{t-1} = \{R_{t-1}, R_{t-2}, \dots\}$,

$$\mu_t = E(R_t \mid \mathcal{F}_{t-1})$$

$$\sigma_t^2 = Var(R_t \mid \mathcal{F}_{t-1}) = E[(R_t - \mu_t)^2 \mid \mathcal{F}_{t-1}]$$


The key idea is that both the expected return and the volatility can change over time.

### Moving Average (MA) Estimates
For the last $n$ observations,

$$\hat{\mu}_{t;n} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n R_{t-i}$$

$$\hat{\sigma}_{t;n}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (R_{t-i} - \hat{\mu}_{t;n})^2$$


MA gives equal weight to the last $n$ returns.

### EWMA Volatility
The exponentially weighted moving average (EWMA) model gives more weight to recent observations:

$$\hat{\sigma}_t^2 = \lambda \hat{\sigma}_{t-1}^2 + (1-\lambda)R_{t-1}^2$$


In practice, RiskMetrics often uses $\lambda = 0.94$ for daily data.

### GARCH(1,1)
One of the most important conditional volatility models is

$$R_t = \mu_t + a_t, \quad a_t = \sigma_t \epsilon_t, \quad \epsilon_t \sim i.i.d.(0,1)$$

$$\sigma_t^2 = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 a_{t-1}^2 + \beta_1 \sigma_{t-1}^2$$


Interpretation:
- $\alpha_0$ is the long-run baseline level.
- $\alpha_1 a_{t-1}^2$ captures the effect of yesterday's shock.
- $\beta_1 \sigma_{t-1}^2$ captures volatility persistence.

### One-Step-Ahead Conditional VaR
If the next-day return is conditionally normal, then a common approximation is

$$VaR_{t+1}(0) \approx V_0 Z_\alpha \hat{\sigma}_{t+1} - V_0 \hat{\mu}_{t+1}$$


If the conditional mean is very small, students often use the simpler exam approximation


$$VaR_{t+1}(0) \approx V_0 Z_\alpha \hat{\sigma}_{t+1}$$


### Quick Example: EWMA Update
**Question:** Yesterday's variance estimate was $0.0004$, yesterday's return was $3\%$, and $\lambda = 0.94$. Find today's EWMA variance and volatility.

**Solution:**


$$\hat{\sigma}_t^2 = 0.94(0.0004) + 0.06(0.03)^2 = 0.000376 + 0.000054 = 0.00043$$


Therefore,


$$\hat{\sigma}_t = \sqrt{0.00043} \approx 0.0207 = 2.07\%$$


So today's conditional volatility is **about $2.07\%$**.

### Quick Example: Conditional VaR
**Question:** Using the volatility above, estimate the 99% one-day VaR for a portfolio worth $\$1{,}000{,}000$. Assume the conditional mean is negligible and $Z_{0.01} = 2.326$.

**Solution:**


$$VaR \approx 1{,}000{,}000 \times 2.326 \times 0.0207 \approx \$48{,}148$$


So the estimated **99% conditional VaR is about $\$48{,}148$**.

## Chapter 8: Practical Methods for Computing VaR

For a large portfolio with many assets, a full covariance-based VaR calculation may be expensive or unstable. This chapter focuses on practical shortcuts.

### Historical Simulation (HS)
If the portfolio return is

$$R_{p,t} = \sum_{i=1}^N w_i R_{i,t}$$


then we can use the historical returns of all assets together with today's portfolio weights to generate historical portfolio returns


$$R_{p,1}, R_{p,2}, \dots, R_{p,T}$$


Then sort them in ascending order and take the empirical $(1-C)$ quantile to estimate VaR.

Advantages:
- simple and intuitive
- automatically keeps the historical dependence structure

Limitations:
- assumes the past is representative of the future
- gives equal weight to very old and very recent observations

### Delta-Normal Method
For a nearly linear portfolio, the change in value can be approximated by a first-order Taylor expansion:

$$\Delta V \approx \Delta^{\prime} \Delta S$$


If the risk-factor change $\Delta S$ is multivariate normal with covariance matrix $\Sigma$, then


$$Var(\Delta V) \approx \Delta^{\prime} \Sigma \Delta$$


and the VaR is approximately


$$VaR \approx Z_\alpha \sqrt{\Delta^{\prime} \Sigma \Delta}$$


### Delta-Gamma Method
For options and other nonlinear products, the linear approximation may be too rough. A better approximation is


$$\Delta V \approx \Delta^{\prime} \Delta S + \frac{1}{2}\Delta S^{\prime}\Gamma\Delta S$$


The gamma term captures curvature and is especially important for options.

### Quick Example: Historical Simulation
**Question:** A $\$2{,}000{,}000$ portfolio has the following toy historical daily returns:

$-3.0\%$, $-2.5\%$, $-1.5\%$, $-1.0\%$, $-0.4\%$, $0.3\%$, $0.5\%$, $0.8\%$, $1.0\%$, $1.2\%$.

Using this toy sample and the nearest-rank rule, estimate the 90% one-day VaR.

**Solution:** The 10% lower-tail return is the worst return, which is $-3.0\%$.


$$VaR = 0.03 \times \$2{,}000{,}000 = \$60{,}000$$


So the **90% historical simulation VaR is $\$60{,}000$**.

### Quick Example: Delta-Normal VaR
**Question:** A portfolio has delta $\Delta = 5{,}000$, meaning the portfolio value changes by about $\$5{,}000$ for a one-unit move in the underlying stock price. If the daily volatility of the stock price change is $2$, estimate the 99% VaR.

**Solution:**


$$\sigma_{\Delta V} = 5{,}000 \times 2 = \$10{,}000$$


Then


$$VaR = 2.326 \times 10{,}000 = \$23{,}260$$


So the **99% delta-normal VaR is $\$23{,}260$**.

## Chapter 9: Extreme Value Approaches

Extreme Value Theory (EVT) focuses on the tails of a distribution. This is important in risk management because the largest losses are exactly what VaR and ES try to measure.

### Why EVT?
- Normal models often underestimate extreme losses.
- CLT describes the center of the distribution, not the tails.
- EVT is built specifically for rare and severe events.

### Distribution of the Minimum Return
If returns are i.i.d. with CDF $F(x)$, then the minimum return $r_{(1)}$ has distribution

$$F_{n,1}(x) = 1 - [1 - F(x)]^n$$


For long positions, the minimum return is important because it corresponds to the worst loss.

### Block Maxima / Minima and the GEV Distribution
In the block maxima approach, divide the sample into blocks and keep only the maximum loss or minimum return from each block.

After normalization, the limiting distribution belongs to the Generalized Extreme Value (GEV) family:


$$H_k(x) = \exp\left(-(1+kx)^{-1/k}\right)$$


Interpretation of the tail index $k$:
- $k > 0$: heavy tail (Frechet type)
- $k = 0$: Gumbel case
- $k < 0$: bounded tail (Weibull type)

### Peaks Over Threshold (POT)
Instead of keeping only one extreme from each block, POT uses all observations above a high threshold $u$.

If $X$ denotes a loss and $Y = X-u$ is the exceedance over the threshold, then for a high enough $u$, $Y$ is approximately modeled by a Generalized Pareto Distribution (GPD).

This is often more efficient than the block maxima method because it uses more tail information.

### POT-Based VaR Formula
If $N$ is the total sample size, $N_u$ is the number of exceedances above threshold $u$, and the GPD parameters are $\xi$ and $\beta$, then a common approximation for the high quantile is

$$VaR_p \approx u + \frac{\beta}{\xi}\left[\left(\frac{N}{N_u}(1-p)\right)^{-\xi} - 1\right]$$


This formula is useful when we want a tail-based estimate of VaR.

### Quick Example: Threshold Exceedances
**Question:** Suppose the loss threshold is $5\%$ and the observed daily losses above the threshold are $6.2\%$, $7.5\%$, and $5.8\%$. What exceedances are modeled in the POT method?

**Solution:** Subtract the threshold from each loss:

- $6.2\% - 5.0\% = 1.2\%$
- $7.5\% - 5.0\% = 2.5\%$
- $5.8\% - 5.0\% = 0.8\%$

So the exceedances are **$1.2\%$, $2.5\%$, and $0.8\%$**.

### Quick Interpretation Question
**Question:** If the estimated tail index is $\xi = 0.25$, what does that tell us?

**Answer:** Since $\xi > 0$, the tail is **heavy**. That means very large losses are more likely than under a normal model, so EVT-based VaR will usually be larger than normal-model VaR.

## Chapter 10: Stress Testing

Stress testing studies losses under unusual but plausible scenarios. It complements VaR because VaR based on recent data may miss structural breaks or unprecedented shocks.

### Basic Stress Testing Framework
Suppose the portfolio depends on risk factors $f_{1,0}, \dots, f_{K,0}$, so the current value is

$$V_0 = V(f_{1,0}, \dots, f_{K,0})$$


Under a stress scenario, the risk factors become


$$f_{i,S} = f_{i,0} + \Delta f_{i,S}$$


and the stressed portfolio value is


$$V_S = V(f_{1,S}, \dots, f_{K,S})$$


For revision, it is often easiest to define the stressed loss as


$$SL = V_0 - V_S$$


Some lecture notes write the change as $\Delta V_S = V_S - V_0$, so in exams always check the sign convention.

### Types of Stress Scenarios
1. **Historical scenario:** replay a real crisis, such as Black Monday or the 2008 financial crisis.
2. **Hypothetical scenario:** design a shock that has not happened but is plausible.
3. **Sensitivity analysis:** change one input, such as correlation or volatility, to see how the portfolio reacts.

### Why Stress Testing Matters
- VaR answers: what loss might happen under normal market conditions?
- Stress testing answers: what happens if the market behaves abnormally?

Both are needed in practice.

### Quick Example: Stress Loss
**Question:** A portfolio is currently worth $\$5{,}000{,}000$. Under a hypothetical shock scenario, its value falls to $\$4{,}550{,}000$. Find the stress loss and the percentage loss.

**Solution:**


$$SL = 5{,}000{,}000 - 4{,}550{,}000 = \$450{,}000$$


The percentage loss is


$$\frac{450{,}000}{5{,}000{,}000} = 0.09 = 9\%$$


So the portfolio suffers a **stress loss of $\$450{,}000$**, which is a **9% loss**.

### Quick Interpretation Question
**Question:** If a risk manager changes the correlation between two markets from $0.2$ to $0.9$ to see how the portfolio loss changes, what kind of stress test is this?

**Answer:** This is **sensitivity analysis**, because one model input is deliberately changed to test how sensitive the portfolio is to that assumption.
